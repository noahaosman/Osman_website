My masters research was primarily focused around developing numerical methods for simulating fluid-structure interaction systems, where the dynamics of the fluid are coupled to the motion of an immersed structure These multiphysics systems are remarkably beautiful and complex — they exhibit a range of spatio-temporal scales and nonlinearities in the fluid and the structure (and the coupling between them).

One of the key bottlenecks to simulation of these systems is the treatment of the stiff diffusive term. In order to maintain numerical stability, this term is traditionally treated implicitly in time, resulting in a computationally expensive linear solve at each time step. In my master's thesis I addressed these challenges by instead treating the stiff diffusive term with a stabilized explicit method known as Runge-Kutta-Chebyshev (RKC). Because RKC is explicit, this method avoids the expensive linear solve associated with implicit methods while still retaining their inherit numerical stability. You can read my thesis here — keep an eye out for the forthcoming paper in the Journal of Computational Physics!

The Malkus-Lorenz chaotic waterwheel is a simple mechanical system designed to demonstrate the unpredictability of chaotic systems.

For a subset of coefficients of axial friction the system is not chaotic. After a short transient phase the rotation of the wheel will fall into periodic oscillations that will remain steady for all time.

However, for larger coefficients of axial friction the system falls into chaos; meaning the motion that will never converge to predictable periodic behavior. While the rotation of the wheel in time (plotted below, green curve) appears entirely random, there is a concealed stability in this motion. The orange curve plotted within the wheel is a phase space plot of angular acceleration vs angular velocity. For non-chaotic regimes a peanut-shaped curve arises. In the chaotic regime a butterfly-shaped curve known as the Lorenz strange attractor appears.

Under the direction of Dr. George Rutherford I designed and wrote a parallel computing simulation of the chaotic waterwheel at Illinois State University using MPI in C++. The code was built with the intention of resolving an observed discrepancy between experimental data and the theoretical behavior predicted by the accepted governing equations. Attached here is a simplified version of that code written in JavaScript. Feel free to experiment with different braking coefficient value inputs! For this simulation the transition to chaos appears to happen at a braking coefficient of approximately 0.11.

This project was inspired by my infallible ability to lose at every chess match I play. It sits in the sweet spot between a Pavlovian-esque training device and just straight up cheating. This fully 3D printed chessboard can sense piece positions, determine the optimal next move via an integrated chess-bot, and limit one of the players to only making that move through the use of TENS unit muscle contractions. If the player attempts to pick up the wrong piece, electrical pulses are applied to extension muscles in order to force the grip open. If the player attempts to set a piece onto an incorrect tile, electrical pulses are applied to flexion muscles in order to force the grip to remain closed. In this way you can be guaranteed to win every chess match you play, while simultaneously ensuring that no one involved will have an semblance of fun.

I worked with a team of my friends at Illinois State University to build a model of the "Monkey and Hunter" thought experiment (see Wikipedia page). In short, due to some simple kinematics, a projectile aimed directly at a hanging object (e.g., a monkey) will always collide if the projectile is fired at precisely the same instance when the object begins to fall.
