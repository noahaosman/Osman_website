My masters research was primarily focused around developing numerical methods for simulating fluid-structure interaction systems, where the dynamics of the fluid are coupled to the motion of an immersed structure These multiphysics systems are remarkably beautiful and complex — they exhibit a range of spatio-temporal scales and nonlinearities in the fluid and the structure (and the coupling between them).

One of the key bottlenecks to simulation of these systems is the treatment of the stiff diffusive term. In order to maintain numerical stability, this term is traditionally treated implicitly in time, resulting in a computationally expensive linear solve at each time step. In my master's thesis I addressed these challenges by instead treating the stiff diffusive term with a stabilized explicit method known as Runge-Kutta-Chebyshev (RKC). Because RKC is explicit, this method avoids the expensive linear solve associated with implicit methods while still retaining their inherit numerical stability. You can read my thesis here — and keep an eye out for a forthcoming paper in the Journal of Computational Physics!

The Malkus-Lorenz chaotic waterwheel is a simple mechanical system designed to demonstrate the unpredictability of chaotic systems.

For a subset of coefficients of axial friction the system is not chaotic — after a short transient phase the rotation of the wheel will fall into periodic oscillations and remain steady for all time.
However, for larger coefficients of axial friction the system will fall into chaotic behavior — the motion of the wheel will never converge to predictable periodic behavior. While the radial position in time (plotted in green in the attached simulation) appears entirely random, there is a concealed stability in this motion. The orange curve plotted within the wheel is a phase space plot of angular acceleration vs angular velocity. For non-chaotic regimes a peanut-shaped curve arises, and in the chaotic regime a butterfly-shaped curve known as the Lorenz strange attractor appears.

Under the direction of Dr. George Rutherford at Illinois State University I designed and wrote a parallelized simulation of the chaotic waterwheel using MPI in C++. The code was built with the intention of resolving an observed discrepancy between experimental data and the theoretical behavior predicted by the accepted governing equations. Attached here is a simplified version of that code written in JavaScript. Please experiment with different braking coefficient value inputs! For this simulation the transition to chaos appears to happen at a coefficient of axial friction approximately equal to 0.11.

This project was inspired by my infallible ability to lose at every chess match I play. The project sits somewhere in the sweet spot between Pavlovian-esque training device and just straight up cheating. This fully 3D printed chessboard can sense piece positions, determine the optimal next move via an integrated chess-bot, and limit one of the players into only making that move through the use of TENS unit muscle contractions. If the player attempts to pick up the wrong piece, electrical pulses are applied to extension muscles in order to force their grip open. If the player attempts to set a piece onto an incorrect tile, electrical pulses are applied to flexion muscles to force their grip closed. In this way you can be guaranteed to win at every chess match you play, while simultaneously ensuring that no one involved has an semblance of fun.

I worked with a team of my friends at Illinois State University to build a model of the "Monkey and Hunter" thought experiment (see Wikipedia page). In short, due to some basic kinematics a projectile aimed directly at a hanging object (e.g., a particularly unlucky monkey) will always collide with that object if the projectile is fired at precisely the same instance that the object begins to fall.
A solenoid was used to suspend the monkey in place, and a Bluetooth signal sent from the projectile launcher to an Arduino was used to trigger its release.
